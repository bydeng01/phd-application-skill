# Professor Fit Analysis: Fei-Fei Li (Stanford)

**Your stated PhD interest:** Natural language processing, specifically multilingual large language models and low-resource languages.

**Bottom line up front:** Professor Fei-Fei Li is one of the most accomplished AI researchers in the world, but she is **not a good supervisor fit for your specific interests**. Her research is in computer vision, embodied AI, and spatial intelligence — not NLP, and certainly not multilingual LLMs or low-resource languages. The mismatch is fundamental, not marginal.

---

## Who she is

Fei-Fei Li is the Sequoia Capital Professor in the Computer Science Department at Stanford and co-director of the Stanford Institute for Human-Centered AI (HAI). She leads the Stanford Vision and Learning Lab (SVL). She is historically best known for creating **ImageNet**, the dataset that catalyzed the deep learning revolution in computer vision. She was named one of TIME's "Architects of AI" (Person of the Year cohort, 2025) and received the Queen Elizabeth Prize for Engineering in 2025.

In 2024 she co-founded and now leads **World Labs**, a "spatial intelligence" startup (valued around $1B+) building 3D world models. In late 2025 World Labs released a preview of **Marble**, a model that reconstructs, generates, and simulates 3D environments from text, images, video, or coarse layouts.

## What she actually works on (grounded in recent work)

- **Spatial intelligence / world models.** Her flagship 2025 essay, *"From Words to Worlds: Spatial Intelligence is AI's Next Frontier,"* argues that machines need to perceive, reason about, generate, and navigate the physical 3D world. Critically, she frames spatial intelligence as **"complementary to language intelligence"** — i.e., a separate axis from the LLM/NLP work you want to do. She has described language-only models as "wordsmiths in the dark."
- **Computer vision and visual reasoning** through the Stanford Vision and Learning Lab.
- **Embodied AI and robotics** (learning agents that act in physical/simulated environments).
- **Ambient intelligence for healthcare** (sensor-based, vision-driven monitoring in clinical settings).

## The fit problem

Your interests and her research point in genuinely different directions:

| Your interest | Her actual focus |
|---|---|
| Multilingual LLMs | 3D world models / spatial reasoning |
| Low-resource languages | Computer vision, embodied AI |
| Text-centric NLP | Vision- and physically-grounded AI |

I searched for any multilingual NLP or low-resource-language publications by her and found **none**. Her own positioning explicitly contrasts spatial intelligence *with* the language-intelligence (LLM) paradigm. Even the language-adjacent work in her lab is grounded in vision (image captioning, visual question answering, vision-language grounding) rather than in multilingual modeling, tokenization for under-resourced languages, cross-lingual transfer, or the linguistic and data-equity questions that define low-resource NLP.

There is one narrow bridge: **multilingual vision-language models** are an active research area, and someone could in principle do low-resource multimodal work that touches both her interests and yours. But that would mean substantially reshaping your stated agenda around vision/multimodality — and even then, low-resource-language and multilingual questions are not where her group concentrates.

## Honest recommendation

If you are committed to multilingual LLMs and low-resource languages, you should look elsewhere. Strong fits for your interests are faculty who explicitly work on multilingual NLP, cross-lingual transfer, and language equity. People and groups worth investigating (verify their current students and availability yourself):

- **Stanford NLP Group** — e.g., Christopher Manning, Dan Jurafsky, Tatsunori Hashimoto, Diyi Yang (work spanning LLMs, sociolinguistics, and language variation).
- **Researchers known specifically for multilingual / low-resource NLP** — e.g., Graham Neubig (CMU), David Ifeoluwa Adelani (low-resource African NLP, e.g., the Masakhane community), Antonios Anastasopoulos (low-resource MT), Sebastian Ruder, and the Masakhane collective.

When you do reach out to potential advisors, target faculty whose **recent papers** (last 2–3 years) actually contain the keywords of your interest — multilingual, cross-lingual, low-resource, tokenization, language equity — rather than relying on overall fame.

## Practical notes

- Li is exceptionally senior, runs a major startup (World Labs), co-directs HAI, and carries heavy external commitments. Even for students whose interests align, day-to-day advising access can be limited and is often shared with co-advisors, postdocs, or senior lab members. This is worth weighing independently of topical fit.
- Admission to her group is extremely competitive and is oriented toward vision/embodied/spatial-intelligence candidates.
