## Research focus

The Doudna lab works on the discovery, mechanism, and application of CRISPR-Cas
systems. Three threads run through the recent work:

1. **Mechanistic structural biochemistry of Cas enzymes** — how Cas9 and Cas12a
   find, interrogate, and cut DNA (target search, R-loop formation, two-step
   capture), with the goal of making editing more efficient and precise.
2. **Delivery of genome editors in vivo** — the lab's translational push, run
   largely through the Innovative Genomics Institute (IGI): engineering vehicles
   (enveloped delivery vehicles / EDVs, and lipid-nanoparticle-packaged Cas9
   ribonucleoproteins) that carry editing machinery to specific tissues in
   living animals.
3. **Therapeutic translation** — moving edits into the clinic for blood
   disorders, the liver/lung, immune cells, and rare disease, increasingly via
   the Danaher-IGI Beacon and the CZI Center for Pediatric CRISPR Cures.

For this applicant (biochemistry background, two years of undergrad research on
**lipid-nanoparticle delivery of CRISPR components**, interest in gene-editing
therapeutics), thread #2 is a near-exact match and #1 is squarely within their
biochemistry training.

## Recent publications (last ~3 yrs, with takeaways)

- **"Rapid two-step target capture ensures efficient CRISPR-Cas9-guided genome
  editing"** — Shi, Al-Sayyad, Wasko, … Doudna. *Molecular Cell*, May 2025.
  Mechanistic study of how Cas9 efficiently captures its DNA target in two
  steps; the kind of biochemistry that feeds back into designing better editors.
- **"Dynamic basis of supercoiling-dependent DNA interrogation by Cas12a via
  R-loop intermediates"** — Aris et al. *Nature Communications*, March 2025.
  Shows how DNA supercoiling shapes Cas12a's target interrogation through R-loop
  intermediates — fundamental enzyme mechanism.
- **"In vivo human T cell engineering with enveloped delivery vehicles"** —
  Hamilton et al. *Nature Biotechnology* (Doudna lab). EDVs package Cas9+gRNA and
  use antibody-antigen targeting to deliver editing machinery selectively;
  demonstrated in humanized mice (turning human T cells into CAR-T cells in vivo)
  with no detectable off-target liver delivery. Directly contrasts EDVs against
  AAV and **LNPs**, which the paper notes are harder to target precisely.
- **"Lung and liver editing by lipid nanoparticle delivery of a stable
  CRISPR-Cas9 ribonucleoprotein"** (Doudna-lab-associated, *Nat. Biotechnol.* /
  bioRxiv preprint line, 2023-2024). LNP delivery of a stabilized Cas9 RNP
  achieving editing in lung and liver — the single most on-point paper for this
  applicant's exact prior experience.

## Research agenda & open problems

The clear trajectory is **delivery and in-vivo therapeutic editing**. Stated and
implied open problems:

- **Tissue-targeted delivery beyond liver.** The lab and IGI explicitly want
  vehicles that reach immune cells, lungs, blood-forming stem cells, and the
  brain — not just the liver (where LNPs naturally accumulate). Targeting
  specificity is the bottleneck they keep returning to.
- **Comparing/combining delivery modalities.** EDVs vs. LNPs vs. AAV — each with
  trade-offs in targeting precision, cargo, immunogenicity, and re-dosing. LNPs
  uniquely allow re-dosing (used in the 2025 infant CPS1 therapy), so improving
  their targeting is a live problem.
- **Stabilizing and packaging RNP cargo** for non-viral delivery.
- **Scaling personalized in-vivo CRISPR** (the "n-of-few" rare disease model
  demonstrated with patient KJ in 2025) into a repeatable platform.

A PhD project on engineering LNPs for extra-hepatic, cell-type-specific delivery
of CRISPR cargo would sit right on this front line.
