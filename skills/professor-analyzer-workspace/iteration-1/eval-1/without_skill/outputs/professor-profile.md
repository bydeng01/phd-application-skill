# Professor Profile: Jennifer A. Doudna (UC Berkeley)

**Prepared for:** A biochemistry applicant with two years of undergraduate research on lipid nanoparticle (LNP) delivery of CRISPR components, interested in gene-editing therapeutics.
**Date:** June 2026

---

## Quick verdict

This is a strong fit on paper. The single biggest scientific bottleneck the Doudna lab is working on right now is **delivery** — getting CRISPR machinery into the right cells in the body — and your undergraduate work sits squarely in that problem space. A candidate who has already spent two years formulating LNPs to carry CRISPR cargo is bringing exactly the hands-on skill set the lab's delivery program needs. The main caveats are competitiveness and structure (see "Things to weigh" below), not relevance.

---

## Who she is and how the lab is organized

Jennifer Doudna is a Professor in Molecular & Cell Biology and Chemistry at UC Berkeley, an HHMI Investigator, and shared the 2020 Nobel Prize in Chemistry for CRISPR-Cas9 genome editing. In January 2025 she received the National Medal of Technology and Innovation, and she received the ACS Priestley Medal in 2025.

Important structural point: Doudna is the founder and a leader of the **Innovative Genomics Institute (IGI)**, a UC Berkeley/UCSF joint institute. Much of the translational and delivery work attributed to "the Doudna lab" happens through IGI, often in collaboration with other PIs there — notably **Ross Wilson** (tissue-specific delivery of genome-editing enzymes) and Berkeley chemists such as Matt Francis. If you join, you will likely be embedded in this collaborative ecosystem rather than a single isolated lab. That is a feature for someone interested in delivery, because the delivery expertise is concentrated there.

---

## What the lab is actually working on now (grounded in recent work)

The lab's recent output clusters around solving the delivery problem for therapeutic genome editing. Relevant, real examples:

1. **Enveloped Delivery Vehicles (EDVs).** Jennifer Hamilton, Doudna, and colleagues developed antibody-targeted, virus-like particles that package Cas9 protein and guide RNA and deliver them to specific cell types. The work was published in *Nature Biotechnology* ("In vivo human T cell engineering with enveloped delivery vehicles," 2024), demonstrating in vivo generation of CAR-T cells in humanized mice and cell-type-targeted editing that avoided off-target hepatocyte delivery. This EDV platform has since spun out into a startup backed by ~$82M, signaling the lab's translational momentum.

2. **Self-delivering ("cell-penetrating") Cas9 for the brain.** Kai Chen and colleagues engineered Cas9 ribonucleoproteins fused to cell-penetrating peptides (and iteratively added nuclear-localization signals) so the protein delivers itself into neurons without a virus or LNP, achieving editing in the mouse striatum (published in *Nature Communications*; preprint on bioRxiv, 2023). This is a non-viral, non-LNP delivery angle for the CNS.

3. **In vivo / clinical translation through IGI.** The IGI-led UC Consortium runs a non-profit Phase 1 trial aiming to directly repair the sickle cell disease mutation (ex vivo, with UCSF Benioff Children's Oakland and UCLA), and IGI researchers contributed to the first bespoke personalized in vivo CRISPR therapy for an infant — designed and delivered in roughly six months. This shows the pipeline from delivery science to patients is real and active.

4. **Broader delivery toolbox.** Across the lab and IGI, delivery strategies under active development include lentiviral approaches, lipid nanoparticles, EDVs, and self-delivering RNPs — explicitly framed by Doudna as the field's rate-limiting step for realizing CRISPR's therapeutic promise.

**Note for honesty:** Doudna's lab also retains deep roots in fundamental CRISPR biology — mechanism, structure, novel Cas enzymes and CRISPR-associated systems, and anti-CRISPR/RNA biology. Delivery and therapeutics are a major thrust but not the entirety of the lab. If you want to be in the delivery/therapeutics sub-program specifically, you should say so explicitly, because the lab is broad.

## Why this is a good fit for you

- **Direct topical overlap.** Your LNP-delivery-of-CRISPR experience maps onto one of the lab's central problems. You are not pivoting fields; you are deepening into the exact bottleneck they care about.
- **Transferable wet-lab skills.** LNP formulation experience implies competence in nucleic acid / RNP handling, encapsulation efficiency and particle characterization, cell-based editing readouts, and quantifying delivery efficiency — all directly reusable whether the lab puts you on LNPs, EDVs, or self-delivering RNPs.
- **Comparative framing advantage.** Because you know LNPs well, you can speak intelligently about where LNPs win and where they fall short (e.g., liver tropism, repeat-dosing immunogenicity, limited cell-type targeting), which is precisely the gap EDVs and engineered RNPs aim to fill. That comparative literacy is genuinely useful to them.
- **Therapeutic motivation aligns with IGI's mission.** Your stated interest in gene-editing therapeutics matches IGI's translational, disease-focused orientation (sickle cell, in vivo therapies).

## Things to weigh honestly

- **Competitiveness.** A Nobel laureate's lab is among the most sought-after in the field. Strong grades, a compelling research narrative, and ideally a publication or substantial poster/talk will matter. Standing out on *fit* (the delivery angle) is your best lever.
- **Mentorship structure.** Large, high-profile labs often mean day-to-day mentoring from senior postdocs/staff rather than the PI. Ask current students about this. The IGI collaboration (e.g., Wilson lab) can be a plus here.
- **Admission path.** Doudna's students typically enter through Berkeley PhD programs (e.g., MCB, or the Chemistry/Biophysics/QB3-affiliated routes) and rotate. Confirm which program(s) feed her lab and apply to the right one; direct admission to a specific lab is not how Berkeley works.
- **Confirm current openings.** Lab capacity and funding for new students vary year to year. A brief, specific email before application season is worth sending.

## What to highlight in outreach

Keep an initial email short (a tight paragraph or two), specific, and grounded in their actual work — not generic praise of CRISPR or the Nobel.

**Lead with the fit, not the fame.** Open with the delivery problem and your concrete experience with it.

Specific things to surface:

1. **Your LNP work, made concrete.** State what you actually did and what you can quantify: cargo type (mRNA? RNP? plasmid?), formulation/characterization methods you ran, editing efficiency or delivery metrics you measured, and any cell types or in vivo models you touched. Concrete numbers and techniques beat adjectives.

2. **Connect to a specific lab project.** Reference one real, recent line of work and explain how your background plugs in. Good options:
   - EDVs (Hamilton et al., *Nat. Biotechnol.* 2024) — you can compare/contrast with LNPs and discuss cell-type targeting.
   - Self-delivering / cell-penetrating Cas9 RNPs for the CNS (Chen et al., *Nat. Commun.*) — frame as a non-LNP delivery modality you'd like to understand alongside LNPs.
   - IGI's translational in vivo / sickle cell program — tie to your therapeutics motivation.
   Pick one or two; do not list everything.

3. **A genuine scientific question.** Show you've read the work by posing one sharp question — e.g., how delivery-platform choice (LNP vs. EDV vs. self-delivering RNP) trades off cell-type specificity, dosing/immunogenicity, and manufacturability for a given indication. This signals you think like a delivery scientist, not just an admirer.

4. **Mention the IGI / collaborators by name where relevant.** Noting Ross Wilson's tissue-specific delivery work (if that's where your interest leans) shows you understand how the lab is actually organized and that you're targeting the delivery program deliberately.

5. **Logistics, briefly.** Confirm you're applying to the appropriate Berkeley PhD program this cycle, and ask whether she anticipates taking rotation students. Keep it low-pressure.

**Tone:** specific, modest, scientifically curious. Avoid overclaiming; if your LNP project had limitations, you can frame them as exactly the problems that drew you to her lab's newer delivery modalities.
