# Jennifer Doudna — UC Berkeley

## Research focus

The Doudna Lab studies the structure, mechanism, and engineering of CRISPR-Cas systems and
RNA biology, and increasingly the **translation of CRISPR into therapeutics**. A central
current thread is **delivery**: getting genome editors (Cas9 mRNA, RNP, or protein) into the
right cells and tissues in living animals. The lab pursues this through two parallel platforms
relevant to the applicant:

1. **Lipid nanoparticle (LNP) delivery of CRISPR** — formulating stable Cas9 RNPs into
   tissue-selective LNPs for in vivo editing (liver, lung).
2. **Enveloped Delivery Vehicles (EDVs)** — engineered virus-derived particles (neither true
   virus nor LNP) that package Cas9 and can be surface-decorated with targeting ligands for
   cell-type-specific in vivo editing.

The lab is anchored at the Innovative Genomics Institute (IGI), which explicitly frames its
mission around making CRISPR therapies affordable and broadly deliverable.

## Recent publications (last ~3 yrs, with takeaways)

- **"Targeted Delivery of Genome Editors in vivo"** — Nature Biotechnology (2026, review/perspective).
  Takeaway: a lab-authored survey of the in vivo delivery problem space — the clearest statement
  of where they think the field (and their own work) is heading. Direct reading for the applicant.
- **"Mechanism-guided engineering of a minimal biological particle for genome editing"** — PNAS (Jan 2025), Ngo W et al.
  Takeaway: rational engineering of a minimal particle to deliver editors — core to the EDV program.
- **"Packaged delivery of CRISPR-Cas9 ribonucleoproteins accelerates genome editing"** — Nucleic Acids Research (Feb 2025), Karp H et al.
  Takeaway: packaging RNPs (rather than mRNA) speeds editing kinetics — relevant to formulation/delivery design.
- **"In vivo human T cell engineering with enveloped delivery vehicles"** — Nature Biotechnology (2024), Hamilton J et al.
  Takeaway: EDVs decorated with targeting ligands edited human T-cells into CAR T-cells in mice
  with humanized immune systems — proof of targeted in vivo blood-cell editing.
- **"Lung and liver editing by lipid nanoparticle delivery of a stable CRISPR–Cas9 ribonucleoprotein"** — Nature Biotechnology (2024).
  Takeaway: a directed-evolution thermostable iGeoCas9 RNP formulated into tissue-selective LNPs
  gave 35–56% editing in mouse liver/lung after IV injection. **This is the single most on-point
  paper for the applicant's LNP background.**
- **"Viral delivery of an RNA-guided genome editor for transgene-free germline editing in Arabidopsis"** — Nature Communications (Mar 2025).
- **"A 'CRISPR' way to visualize RNA in live cells"** — Nature Biotechnology (Feb 2025), Xia C et al. (RNA-imaging branch, less relevant to the applicant).

## Research agenda & open problems

- **Cell-type-specific in vivo delivery** — moving beyond liver (the "easy" LNP tissue) to
  targeted editing of T-cells, brain, and other tissues. EDV ligand-targeting and LNP tropism
  engineering are both active.
- **Brain delivery for neurodegeneration** — developing cell-penetrant Cas9 RNPs for the adult
  mouse brain, with Huntington's disease as a stated therapeutic target.
- **Manufacturability & cost** — IGI-framed goal of driving CRISPR-therapy cost down via
  scalable, in vivo (vs. ex vivo) delivery.
- **Likely next papers:** improved LNP tropism / targeting ligands, EDV payload and targeting
  expansion, and in vivo CNS or immune-cell editing demonstrations.

  willingness to learn the EDV/particle-engineering side.

  immunology — all heavily used here. Not disqualifying, but areas to acknowledge/learn.
- Berkeley MCB is **rotation-based and committee-admitted**: fit with Doudna does not by itself
  secure admission; the application must satisfy the department, and lab placement happens after
  rotations.
