**Test Applicant — PhD in Sim-to-Real Dexterous Manipulation**

When I read Dr. O'Keefe's 2025 paper "Tactile-Guided Sim-to-Real for In-Hand Manipulation," one idea reframed the problem I had spent two years working on: that the sim-to-real gap can be closed not only by randomizing what a robot *sees* but by randomizing what it *feels*. My own work has attacked that gap from the vision-and-geometry side. Crossing to the tactile side — and toward in-hand re-orientation that generalizes across object shapes — is exactly the direction I want my PhD to take, and Dr. O'Keefe's lab is where that problem is being defined.

My research has centered on making policies trained in simulation work on real hardware. As a graduate researcher in the Patel Robot Learning Lab, I built a MuJoCo-to-real pick-and-place pipeline and designed curriculum domain-randomization schedules to drive transfer. On a UR5 arm, the resulting policy reached 88% success on a six-object set evaluated entirely on the real robot — a result that taught me how much the *schedule* of randomization, not just its presence, governs whether a policy survives the reality gap. That finding is the spine of my MSc thesis, "Domain Randomization Schedules for Sim-to-Real Transfer in Pick-and-Place," and of my CoRL workshop paper, "Curriculum Domain Randomization for Sim-to-Real Manipulation." Earlier, as an undergraduate, I implemented PPO baselines for a grasping benchmark, which is where I first started caring about manipulation rather than RL in the abstract.

What I keep returning to is a limit of my own approach: I have randomized vision and geometry, but the hardest manipulation problems — in-hand re-orientation, dexterous regrasping — are governed by contact, and contact is where my current pipelines are blind. This is precisely the open problem Dr. O'Keefe's work foregrounds: how to randomize *tactile* signals effectively, rather than visual or geometric ones, and how to fine-tune dexterous policies sample-efficiently on real hardware. My background gives me a running start on the second half of that question — I have built and debugged real-robot evaluation loops and curriculum-randomization schedules — while the first half, tactile randomization, is the genuinely new skill I want to acquire and contribute to. The 2024 "Learning Dexterous Policies with Sparse Tactile Feedback" work suggests the lab already thinks carefully about how little tactile signal a policy can get away with, which connects directly to my interest in sample-efficient real-world learning.
