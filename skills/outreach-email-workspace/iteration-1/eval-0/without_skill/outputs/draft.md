# Cold Email Draft — Prof. Sergey Levine (UC Berkeley)

## IMPORTANT: Read this before sending

Professor Levine's faculty page explicitly states he will **not reply to PhD
admissions emails** and directs prospective students to apply to the Berkeley
EECS PhD program and list him as a faculty member of interest.

**What this means for you:**

- A cold email asking "can I do a PhD in your lab?" will almost certainly go
  unanswered, and a poorly-judged one (ignoring his stated policy) can leave a
  mildly negative impression. The real decision happens through the formal
  Berkeley EECS PhD application, which is read by an admissions committee, not
  through email.
- So the honest advice is: **the single most important action is to submit a
  strong Berkeley EECS PhD application and name him.** The email below is
  optional and is written to respect his policy rather than fight it.
- If you do send something, keep it short, do not ask him to reply, do not ask
  for a meeting or a funding commitment, and make it genuinely useful (i.e.,
  pointing him to a concrete piece of your work). Think of it as a low-cost
  flag, not a request. Many strong applicants send nothing and simply apply.

A reasonable alternative to emailing Levine directly is to reach out to his
**senior PhD students or postdocs** whose recent papers you build on — they are
more responsive and often have informal input on incoming students.

## Notes on what I changed and why

- **Respect the stated policy up front.** Both versions open by acknowledging
  he doesn't answer admissions email and explicitly say no reply is needed.
  This is the difference between looking like you did your homework and looking
  like you spam-blasted faculty.
- **No funding ask.** You need full funding, but raising it in a cold email is
  premature and reads as transactional. Berkeley EECS PhD admits are funded
  through the program (RA/TA/fellowship); funding is settled through admission,
  not negotiated by email. Leave it out.
- **No meeting / call request.** He hasn't invited it, and asking contradicts
  the "no reply needed" framing.
- **Specificity is your only real asset here.** Naming HiL-SERL and pi0.5 and
  connecting them to your own sim-to-real / real-robot experience is what makes
  this not generic. Make sure any paper you cite, you can actually discuss.
- **Fill the brackets and attach/link a CV.** If your workshop paper has a URL,
  include it — a concrete artifact is worth more than adjectives.
- **Bottom line:** put your energy into the application itself (statement of
  purpose, letters, listing him correctly). The email is, at best, a small
  positive signal and, at worst, ignored — which he told you in advance.
